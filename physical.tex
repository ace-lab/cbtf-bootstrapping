\section{Physical Configuration}

\subsection{Computer Lab? Open Space? Whose?}
\lesson{Not all available lab spaces are created equal.}

We used existing, shared, centrally-managed computer labs for our proto-CBTF.
Because they were already computer labs, we knew they had sufficient power and
networking for many computers.  If you're  expecting to
furnish/equip an empty space, be sure it has enough power and
wired Ethernet capacity for your expected load (don't rely on wi-fi for something
as mission-critical as an exam) and a way to run the necessary cabling
to each computer.
In addition, a computer lab is likely already occupancy-rated to
be densely filled with students; some otherwise-promising large
open spaces may not be.

Similarly, because the shared labs are centrally administered, cleaned, and
maintained,
there were
well-known mechanisms for notifying the maintainers
of equipment failures.  If you're doing it yourself, someone needs to
be responsible for this maintenance.

Students and proctors must also have physical access to the CBTF (and the
building containing it) during operating hours.
In our case, the labs are only open to
students when they are staffed by a particular instructor or academic
staff member who has reserved the lab in connection with a particular
course.
Since we had a rotating staff of TAs (proctors) who needed access, we
had to work out a system by which they could obtain and return the
physical keys before and after each exam shift.
(Many spaces on our campus are controlled by cardkey access that can
be enabled for particular students' ID cards on a time-window basis,
but these labs had physical keys.)


\subsection{Accessibility (a11y)}

\lesson{Approach your disabled students program office early, but message
  clearly that although the CBTF can handle only a subset of
  exam-related accommodations,  all students in your courses will
  still get the accommodations they need.}

%% \lesson{There are more accommodations in heaven and earth
%%   that are dreamt of in your philosophy.}

PrairieLearn's a11y compliance is good enough to satisfy our very
rigorous campus a11y review, and it had been easy to confirm the a11y
status of the shared labs, since they were centrally managed.  For
example, we knew they were accessible and navigable by wheelchair, but
as currently furnished they lacked standing desks or adjustable-height desks.
But we knew that a11y went far beyond software and physical accessibility,
so we approached our Disabled Students
Program office (DSP hereafter) as early as possible as potential collaborators.
DSP has a proctoring facility for students needing
exam accommodations, but it is vastly oversubscribed.  Our goal had been 
to convey the message that by handling ``common''
accomodations such as reduced distraction and extra time on exams,
which collectively account for more than 80\% of all exam-related
accommodation requests, we could
could relieve the DSP proctoring center of much workload.

However, we initially received a cool reception
since DSP was quite apprehensive about the idea of a CBTF
serving an entire course.
In retrospect, we believe one possible reason was that our proposal
and worldview were very far away from their
usual operating model (see~\cite{cbtf-not-proctoring} for more detail
on this point), so they may have initially assumed that we were either
trying to co-opt their responsibilities or trying to incompetently
scale up what they were already doing.
They initially insisted, for
example, that every CBTF seat must meet a wide range of a11y criteria, whereas
our goal was to identify which accommodations
could be met straightforwardly, and continue handling the rest by
one-off means.
It took a while for the two sides to understand each other
better---having a manifesto such as~\cite{cbtf-not-proctoring}  in
place might have accelerated that understanding---but as of February 2026, over
85\% of all exam-related accommodations 
 approved by DSP can be handled
within our CBTF.
  
In addition to extra time on exams, the second most common exam
accommodation at our institution involves a ``reduced distraction''
exam environment.
We were dismayed that we couldn't find a canonical definition
of the physical parameters of this accommodation 
within our institution, let alone systemwide across the several
campuses in our state's public higher education system.
In the end, our departmental liaison to the DSP office (our department
is large enough to have one) developed
a configuration involving headphones and view partitions that
DSP agreed would satisfy the RD requirement for most
students, with the caveat that students who had more stringent needs
(e.g. ``must take the exam alone in a room'') would still be
accommodated, just not in the CBTF.

\subsection{Privacy}

We purchased anti-glare/privacy screens for about \$30 per
workstation\footnote{\url{https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0C9L8NQLW}} that
can be affixed to monitors using Velcro, since we could not make
permanent changes to the shared computers.
When it was the CBTF's turn to use the labs, we would arrange for our
reservation to begin (and proctors to arrive) 30 minutes before the
first reservable exam shift to put the screens on,  then stay 30 min
late to take them off.
This implies the CBTF also needs a place where this and related
supplies can be stored.
In our case, the other supplies included a few pairs of
noise-cancelling headphones, and packs of scratch paper color-coded
for different sessions.

We also purchased inexpensive desk
dividers\footnote{\url{https://www.amazon.com/Dividers-Students-Waterproof-Classroom-Supplies/dp/B0F3CVPDYC}}
both to further thwart cheating and provide some visual blocking for
Reduced Distraction accommodations (see Section~\ref{sec:a11y}).

You will want a way for students to leave personal belongings (smart
watches, phones, etc.) clearly away from testing stations.  Our shared
labs didn't have any good place for this, so we had students leave
their belongings under the desk; but it's harder to spot them
fidgeting with things.  Similarly, beware of items in bulky jacket
pockets when jackets aren't removed, baggy sweaters, and so on.

A major caveat to ``no devices'' is two-factor authentication; see Section~\ref{sec:networking}.
