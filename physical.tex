\section{Physical Configuration}


\subsection{Computer Lab? Or Open Space? Whose?}
\lesson{If you need to leave shared space the way you found it, 
   account for all setup and teardown needs.}

We were
determined to find labs with centrally-managed hardwired computers, rather than
using student laptops with Wi-Fi in a classroom: that is a viable use
case, but a scenario we simply do not trust for high-stakes exams.
Our departmental
computer labs, though underutilized since
most students own laptops, were scheduled in a 
coarse-grained and non-transparent way that made
them hard to ``rent'' as CBTF space.
In contrast, the two instructional labs we were able to schedule part-time
(Figure~\ref{fig:spaces}) were centrally operated, which 
meant there were mature and centralized
procedures for scheduling the rooms, gaining access, and handling IT
and  physical plant maintenance concerns.
The access protocol required whoever had reserved the lab
to borrow a physical key, 
so we had to work out a system by which our rotating staff of proctors could obtain and return the
physical keys before and after each exam shift.
If you're planning on running evening exams, be sure the building
containing the labs is accessible to students after hours.
And if you're planning to fill an empty room with desks and computers,
keep in mind that the room may or may not have sufficient
power,
wired networking, or fire-safety occupancy allowance to serve as a CBTF.

Since we couldn't make permanent changes to the shared-space
facilities, we purchased Velcro-attachable anti-glare/privacy screens for
each workstation\footnote{\url{https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0C9L8NQLW}};
our proctors would  arrive 30 minutes before the
first reservable exam shift to attach them, then stay a bit later to
remove them.



A shared CBTF therefore also needs a place where such
supplies can be stored when others use the space.  Besides the above,
we bought a few pairs of
noise-cancelling headphones and various packs of scratch paper color-coded
for different sessions.

We  needed a way for students to keep personal belongings (smart
watches, phones, etc.)  away from testing workstations.  Our shared
labs didn't have any good place for this, so we had students stow
their belongings under the desk, where unfortunately it's harder to
spot students fidgeting with them.
Similarly, beware of items in bulky sweater or jacket
pockets when those garments aren't removed; we've even heard stories
of students gluing cheat sheets to the undersides of baseball-cap visors.  In
the permanent spaces we later obtained, we installed dedicated racks
for students to leave all personal effects.
(A major caveat to ``no devices'' is two-factor
authentication, as Section~\ref{sec:networking} describes.)


\subsection{Accessibility (a11y)}

\lesson{Accommodations definitions and procedures are highly bespoke.
  Approach your student accommodations program office early and understand
  how your shared responsibility for handling accommodations will
  impact existing workflows.}

PrairieLearn's a11y compliance is exemplary, as are the physical a11y
affordances of our shared labs.
But exam accommodations transcend software and physical a11y,
so we approached our Student Accommodations Center (a generic term for
the campus unit that serves students with disabilities that may impact
their educational experience; SAC hereafter) early, as potential
collaborators and advisors to help with design elements such as the
Reduced Distraction stations.
SAC has a proctoring facility for students needing
exam accommodations, but it is vastly oversubscribed.  
We tried to convey the message that by handling ``common''
accomodations such as reduced distraction and extra time,
which collectively account for more than 80\% of all exam-related
accommodation requests at our institution, we could
could relieve the SAC proctoring center of much workload.
However, 
SAC was initially quite apprehensive about the idea of a CBTF
serving an entire course.
In retrospect, we believe one possible reason was that our whole-class
proctoring proposal
and our model of running exams were very far away from their
usual operating model, so they may have initially assumed that we were
trying to either
co-opt their responsibilities or incompetently
scale up what they were already doing.

The second-most-common exam-related accommodation at our institution
is ``reduced distraction (RD) environment.''
We were surprised and dismayed to discover that there was no precise definition
of the physical parameters of this accommodation, and
that practices for handling it seemed to vary
from course to course, the most widely used practice being to
underfill a room to 30--50\% capacity.
For our pilot course's final exam, we designated a few CBTF sessions
as RD and restricted reservations for those sessions to students with
an RD accommodation (Section~\ref{sec:reservations} describes why this turned
out to be needlessly painful).
In the end, it took the intervention of our departmental liaison to
the SAC office, who had a good relationship with them, 
to help develop
a configuration involving headphones and inexpensive view
partitions\footnote{\url{https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0F3CVPDYC}} that
SAC agreed would satisfy the RD requirement for most
students.
Despite the low cost of these dividers, we configured only a few stations
this way (based on historical data for students in the pilot course
requiring this accommodation) since the dividers make it more
difficult for proctors to spot suspicious behaviors.

Another challenge was our institution's accommodations-handling
workflow, which we have since learned is neither better nor worse than
that of other institutions---they are just all bespoke.
PrairieTest has robust accommodation-handling facilities, so that (for
example) students with extra-time accommodations receive the extra
time automagically, and those with reduced-distraction or
assistive-technology accommodations are steered to designated test
stations designed to meet those needs.
But PrairieTest was designed around a workflow in which students
receive an accommodation letter from the SAC and present it directly
to CBTF staff to note the accommodations, something course instructors
cannot do in the current PrairieTest software.

However, when a student at our institution gets an accommodation,
the information is provided \emph{only\/} to
the student's instructor(s), who must then convey it to CBTF
staff for entry into PrairieTest.
But CBTF staff cannot enter accommodations until
the student has signed into PrairieTest at least once---and if the 
student has done so for the purpose of making their first exam
reservation (not an unusual scenario), \emph{a~fortiori\/} that reservation will not reflect
their accommodations because CBTF staff have been unable to enter
them.
This Kafkaesque process has caused numerous problems for students and
faculty;
as of August 2026, PrairieTest's workflows are being made more flexible,
partially in response to concerns such as ours.
