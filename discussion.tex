\subsection{Funding}

\subsection{Exam Locations, Times, and Durations}

Our pilot final exam was heavily based on existing paper exams for
the course, which had a baseline 3 hour time limit for the final.
For the pilot we adjusted it to 2:50 to allow for the 10 minute
check-in, but as we have moved away from pilot and intro production,
we only support 1-hour (really 50-minute) quizzes during the semester
and only 2-hour (really 110-minute) finals, as a compromise for being
able to manage reservations scalably.  \cite{cbtf-not-proctoring}
presents the justification for this choice, which in practice hasn't
been much of a hardship.

Some institutions have regulations related to when/where students can
take exams.  Be sure you are familiar with and complying with yours.
For example, the summer offering of our pilot course had previously
been 100\% online and asynchronous, including online remote exams;
since converting to CBTF exams (remote exams are now possible only due
ato a documented disability exception), we can no longer offer the
summer version as a fully online course.  As instructors, we were more
than happy to pay this price in exchange for higher academic integrity
and saner exam administration, but the Summer Sessions program office
was less thrilled since a course with required in-person exams draws a
smaller audience and therefore less revenue than a fully-online course.
Fortunately, at our institution, shared governance is the rule and
faculty usually have final discretion over such matters, sometimes
after clearing it with a Faculty Senate-staffed curriculum committee
that oversees changes to course format descriptions.

For your pilot course, do what you have to do; but it may be worth
starting the long process (and at a large public 
institution, it will be long) of putting some language in place
permanently that addresses some of these questions.  For example, at
our institution, a course is classified as either having a ``written final
exam'' or not; if it does, the exam is assigned a specific, non-negotiable time and room(s) 
during finals week, based on projected course enrollment.
We are now pursuing addenda to the academic policies manual to address
CBTF-specific questions such as: are CBTF exams
considered ``written'' exams for the purposes of courses that specify
a written final exam?  Since CBTF exams are by nature multi-day,
may they be offered over a window that overlaps the ``official'' final
exam slot for the course, or even
over a window that \emph{doesn't\/} overlap the official slot, since
that course now no longer requires general-allocation classroom (or
gymnasium) space to be allocated for its final?  Can they be
offered outside of the official final exam period, as they might be
for students who have a travel conflict?  And so on.

These administrative minutiae, along with determining the number of angels that can
dance on the head of a pin, we leave to discussions between you and
your central campus administration.  For a bootstrap, we suggest clearing your plan
with your students, and asking forgiveness
rather than permission if you end up accidentally transgressing the
letter of the law.  Our experience was that the vast majority of
students greatly appreciated the flexibility of being able to schedule
their final exam time.\footnote{Indeed, in a follow-up survey with a much larger
($n=~750$) upper-division course that used the CBTF in Summer 2025,
this flexibility was the most frequently cited benefit among students
who preferred the CBTF to traditional paper based exams.  48\%
definitely preferred the CBTF, 30\% definitely preferred paper, and
22\% had no strong preference.}

accommodations until after CBTF staff (not the course instructor) have
entered those accommodations.  But CBTF staff cannot enter those accommodations until
the student has signed into PrairieTest at least once.  And
instructors, who are the sole recipients of accommodation letters,
cannot enter CBTF-related student accommodations  
at all.  This Kafkaesque process has resulted in many students having
to cancel and rebook a previously-made reservation so that it
correctly reflects their accommodations.

All of that said, the value of being able to handle the majority (over
87\% today) of exam-related accommodations within the CBTF framework
has been an enormous benefit.
In our case, the CBTF can handle extra time, reduced distraction, bathroom
breaks during exams, and a few others, and we will soon accommodate
standing desks and adjustable-height desks.
Students whose accommodations \emph{cannot\/} be met by the
CBTF must be handled by either course staff or the DSP proctoring
center, but that is a far smaller number of students.

Our experience from talking to colleagues is that different campuses
have different policies as to who may see accommodations information
and how it is routed, so the only advice we can give is to get far in
front of this and have a known process in place for conveying
CBTF-relevant accommodations to whoever is in charge of managing them
at exam time.

\subsection{A Cautionary Tale About Cheating}
\label{sec:cheating}

For the remote PrairieLearn quizzes mentioned in the Background section,
students were required to setup
their equipment at home in a such a way that they were both capturing their
activity as a screencast, and had a separate second camera (such as a
cell phone) recording video of the student taking the exam, with 
the student's face and hands visible. Students had to submit both the
screencast and the live video.

After the Fall~2023 final exam, we found one student who did much more
poorly on the final than their performance on the quizzes would
predict.  After a combination of programmatic and manual forensics, we
were able to determine that the student had had someone else take the
quizzes for them, and submitted screencast video that did not match
the ``live'' video of them taking the quiz, on not one but several
quizzes.  The student was disciplined, but we learned a humbling and
discouraging lesson about the brazenness and length to which some students
would go to cheat.  Since that incident, \emph{all\/} summative
assessments for that course are in-CBTF only except for medical
exemptions, and as of Spring 2026, we have had only one confirmed
attempt at in-CBTF cheating (in another course).  Many of our
colleagues had never fully trusted remote exams in the first place;
the final nail has now been driven into that coffin.
