\section{Operations: Staffing and Messaging}
\label{sec:ops}

\subsection{Staffing}

\lesson{Student and faculty expectations are quickly set and hard
  to change, so operate ``at scale'' from day one.}

In the UIUC model, proctors are there to invigilate, not to answer questions about
the exams.  This makes proctors interchangeable across courses and frees costly
TAs from proctoring duties.
Importantly, even though our course TAs did participate in proctoring
for the pilot, we adhered to the ``no questions'' practice to avoid setting a
scale-thwarting precedent.
Instead of asking proctors a question,  students make a note on the
exam that instructors review later, using a PrairieLearn
feature provided for this purpose.
In our case, our exam was spread over ten sessions over four days;
some students in the first session actually found a
bug in the exam that we fixed in time for all subsequent sessions.
This would have been impossible with a paper exam.

We initially believed that one
proctor would suffice for our
small labs, but we quickly learned otherwise.  First, proctors'
attention span is finite: after a couple of hours, they need a break,
so you need multiple proctors even if you have only one room to staff.
Second, if a proctor is late or absent,
it impacts a lot of students, so redundancy is essential: we quickly
changed to two proctors per session,
which requires
larger CBTF rooms to make the economics attractive.  As we seek out
additional CBTF space on campus, 
we're uninterested in any space smaller than about 60 seats.

We also quickly realized that as soon as we needed proctors beyond
our TAs (for example, for other courses that joined our CBTF effort
after Fall 2023), those proctors need supervision, consistent
training and onboarding, and so on.  As a stopgap, we borrowed
existing IT staff time to fill a part-time
``CBTF manager'' position who oversaw both IT-related and proctor/HR
related issues.  
Proctors must pass character references related to
responsibility, punctuality, and so on, but have no academic
duties or connections to the courses using the CBTF.
Today, CBTF hiring and operations is a full-time job homed in our instructional
technology unit, staffed by a professional
experienced in both IT and help desk
management.

\subsection{Communication With Students}

\lesson{Repeat every CBTF procedure and policy multiple times via
  multiple channels with painstaking clarity.}

CBTF exams were new for most students.
They quickly understood the concept of making a reservation to take
an exam, and appreciated the flexibility.
Nonetheless, the most important policy messages for students, which
continue to be an issue as we have scaled up our CBTF efforts, are as
follows:

\begin{itemize}

\item If you procrastinate making a reservation, and
when you finally get around to it none of the
available slots work for your schedule, that is your problem, not our
problem.

\item If you make a reservation, fail to show up without properly cancelling
and rescheduling, and are then unable to find any remaining slots
within the exam window, that is your problem, not our problem.

\end{itemize}

In practice, we needed to define ``your problem'' to
mean ``a situation that causes friction for you and/or the course
staff, rather than for CBTF staff.''
In particular, we should have been more firm in stating to faculty
that in these cases, the burden of proctoring the student (in the
classroom, remotely, or however), will once again devolve onto them
and their course staff.

This last point bears reemphasizing.
Many faculty are not great at emphatically communicating important
procedures to students; many students are not great at retaining the
message when they first hear it.
By not being firm enough in our wording and repetitive enough in
nagging faculty to convey the message through multiple channels
(course home page, email announcement, posting to course question
boards, asking TAs to announce during office hours, and so on), we are
still suffering from higher rates than we'd like of students in the
above two scenarios who end up pushing a lot of extra work onto our
CBTF staff, thwarting scalability.
Because the CBTF staff were so accommodating and customer-focused (in
the best possible way) from the start, students grew accustomed to not
suffering ill effects from being lax.
We are redesigning our communication and doubling down on a
structured and repetitive ``phased program'' of communication to
students, to faculty, and to students through faculty, to neutralize
this operational challenge.

The mechanics of getting to and using the CBTF need to be repeated
multiple times as well.
Many pilot students had never needed to use
the shared labs, and since the labs were in a building that rarely hosts CS
courses, some students had difficulty locating it
despite the pictures and maps we posted on the course homepage, and 
were late for exam reservations. 
Similarly, make sure building access (for both students and proctors)
is not an issue:
On one day where we ran evening exams,  we had to post
an extra proctor inside the building door because after 6:00PM the building required cardkey
access that most students didn't have.

Within the CBTF, post clear and visible instructions on the mechanics of logging
in to access an exam, checking in with proctors, and so on.
Initially we had preprinted slips of paper that we handed out as people
entered the room, since we could not affix anything permanent to the
walls or doors.

The students in our pilot course had already used PrairieLearn
(for remote quizzes), but if yours
haven't, an high-stakes exam shouldn't be their first CBTF experience.
Today we have a 20-minute orientation quiz that we strongly
urge all students to take if they've never used the CBTF before. Its
content covers CBTF policies, and taking
it requires them to go through the mechanics of 
making a reservation, locating and navigating to the
CBTF, and so on.  As a backup, the remote version of the same quiz
remains open all semester, so students who didn't do a dry run can
still be exposed to PrairieLearn and the IT aspects of CBTF operations.
