\section{Operations}


\subsection{Staffing}

\lesson{Operate from day one as if you had many courses and many proctors.}

In the UIUC model, proctors are there to invigilate, not to answer questions about
the exams.  This makes proctors interchangeable across courses and frees (expensive)
TAs from proctoring duties.
Importantly, even our course staff participated in proctoring
for the pilot, we adhered to this practice, to avoid setting a
scale-thwarting precedent.
Instead,  students make a note on the exam (PrairieLearn
has a specific feature for this), which can be reviewed later by
instructors. In our case, the first batch of students actually found a
bug in the exam that we managed to fix for all subsequent batches.

We initially believed one
proctor---a TA reporting to the instructors---would suffice for the
small labs we had, but we quickly learned otherwise.  First, proctors'
attention span is finite: after a couple of hours, they need a break.
Second, if a proctor is late or absent,
it impacts a lot of students, so redundancy is essential: we quickly
changed to two proctors per room,
which in turn requires
larger testing rooms to make the economics attractive.  As we seek out
additional permanent space on campus, 
we're essentially uninterested in any space smaller than about 60 seats.

We also quickly realized that as soon as we needed proctors beyond
our TAs (for example, for other courses that joined our CBTF effort
after Fall 2023), those proctors need supervision, consistent
training and onboarding, and so on.  As a stopgap, we had a de facto
``CBTF manager'' position who oversaw both IT-related and proctor/HR
related issues.
Proctors must pass character references related to
responsibility, punctuality, and so on, but have no academic
duties or connections to the courses using the CBTF.
Today, the CBTF manager is a full-time job homed in our instructional
technology unit.


\subsection{Communication With Students}

\lesson{Give crystal clear
instructions, multiple times, on every CBTF procedure and policy.
If possible, do a comprehensive dry run.}

CBTF exams were new for most students.
They quickly understood the concept of making a reservation to take
an exam, and appreciated the flexibility.
Nonetheless, the most important policy messages for students, which
continue to be an issue as we have scaled up our CBTF efforts, are as
follows:

\begin{itemize}

\item If you procrastinate making a reservation, and
the result is that when you finally get around to it none of the
available slots work for your schedule, that is your problem, not our
problem.

\item If you make a reservation, fail to show up without cancelling
and rescheduling, and are then unable to find any remaining slots
within the exam window, that is your problem, not our problem.

\end{itemize}

In practice, we needed to define ``your problem'' to
mean ``a situation that causes friction for you and/or the course
staff, rather than for the CBTF itself.''
In particular, we should have been more firm in stating to faculty
that in these cases, the burden of proctoring the student (in the
classroom, remotely, or however), will once again devolve onto them
and their course staff.

This last point bears reemphasizing.
Many faculty are not great at emphatically communicating important
procedures to students; many students are not great at retaining the
message when they first hear it.
By not being firm enough in our wording and repetitive enough in
nagging faculty to convey the message through multiple channels
(course home page, email announcement, posting to course question
boards, asking TAs to announce during office hours, and so on), we are
still suffering from higher rates than we'd like of students in the
above two scenarios who end up pushing a lot of extra work onto our
CBTF staff, thwarting scalability.
We are redesigning our communication and doubling down on a
structured and repetitive ``phased program'' of communication to
students, to faculty, and to students through faculty, to neutralize
this operational challenge.

The mechanics of getting to and using the CBTF need to be repeated
multiple times as well.
For example, many of our students had never had occasion to use one of
the shared labs, as it was in a building that rarely hosts computer
science courses; some students had difficulty locating it
despite the pictures and maps we posted on the course homepage, and so
were late for their exam reservation. 
Similarly, make sure building access (for both students and proctors)
is not an issue.
On one day where we ran evening exams,  we had to post
a proctor at the building door because after 6:00PM the building required cardkey
access that most students didn't have.  (Getting one-off building access for a
few proctors wasn't too hard; getting it for 230 students would have been
a bureaucratic nightmare.)

Within the CBTF, be crystal clear on the mechanics of logging
in to access an exam, checking in with proctors, and so on.
Initially we had preprinted slips of paper that we handed out as people
entered the room. 

Our students were already familiar with PrairieLearn because they had
used it for the remote quizzes during the semester, but if yours
aren't, an in-CBTF high-stakes exam shouldn't be their first exposure
to it.  Today we have a 20-minute orientation quiz that we strongly
urge all students to take if they've never used the CBTF before: its
content covers CBTF policies rather than course materials, but taking
it requires them to go through the mechanics of logging into
PrairieTest, making a reservation, locating and navigating to the
CBTF, and so on.  As a backup, the at-home version of the same quiz
remains open all semester, so students who didn't do a dry run can
still be exposed to the CBTF policies.
