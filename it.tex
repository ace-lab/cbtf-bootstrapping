\subsection{Software: Don't Self-Host}
\label{sec:software}
\lesson{Don't self-host your exam software; the expected cost
  savings evaporates with other overheads.}



Although 
Canvas\footnote{instructure.com/canvas} is our official
campuswide Learning Management System, we found it impoverished in
important ways impacting both assessment authoring and assessment
delivery (Section~\ref{sec:networking} describes the latter).
In contrast, we found much to admire about the
PrairieLearn\footnote{prairielearn.org} assessment authoring and
delivery system and intended to use it.
Because our pilot consisted of only one course actually trying to run CBTF-like exams
and a few others that would be using PrairieLearn for formative assessments only,
we initially hosted PrairieLearn ourselves, since it was open source
and we didn't know if we'd need to make changes to integrate with our
campus systems.  In retrospect, this was a mistake.

Our IT staff are highly competent and very cooperative.  However,
this represented a brand-new set of tools and
workflows for them, and their time and budget are tight.
Even with light usage, smooth operation during exams is mission-critical.
As soon as more courses began doing CBTF quizzes,
the manual scaling of cloud resources to
ensure sufficient capacity quickly became a nuisance, with IT staff
needing a 24-hour heads up before any scale-up period.  PrairieLearn
hosting would have made this transparent.

Cloud provisioning was further complicated by having to managee Docker
containers for custom autograders, on which our CS course relied heavily (another reason to have PrairieLearn host for you, although
as a caveat, a course requiring custom external grading containers costs more
than 2x per seat as one that doesn't).

Finally, the open-source software does not include PrairieTest,
which facilitates
scheduling the CBTF itself and allowing students to make exam
reservations.
Again, we assumed that with only one course needing exam scheduling
and two shared lab spaces, we'd be able to use free tools such as
SignupGenius in lieu of PrairieTest.  This was also a mistake.
Even ``simple'' exam scheduling quickly becomes complicated when one
doesn't use purpose-built tools.
First, students with extra-time accommodations must be handled
manually.
Second, students with reduced-distraction accommodations must be
handled separately.  Traditionally, this is done by filling
a room to only $1/3$ or $1/2$ capacity, so we dedicated separate
CBTF sessions to this purpose; but we had to manually assign students
to the correct sessions, whereas PrairieLearn would have allowed us to
simply configure certain sessions as allowing or disallowing
reservations for  particular
categories of students.

seats during one particular shift over a 4-day window during finals week.

\subsection{Network: Not Just Firewalls}
\label{sec:networking}

\lesson{Delivering assessments securely requires a secure
  LMS---firewalls alone aren't enough.}

Since each instructional lab had its own subnet, it was easy to
configure  the campus-level firewall to
lock out most of the
Internet when accessed from one of the labs, except for the servers
needed to access PrairieLearn exams. 
One
caveat regarding this configuration was that attempting to access
any blocked site resulted in the same error message one would see if
there were no Internet connection at all.
Some students would make a typo in accessing PrairieLearn or
PrairieTest, see this error, and report to the proctor that their
computer had no Internet.  (This occurred numerous times despite the
fact that upon signing in, 
there was an immediate and obvious desktop
shortcut that students were instructed to use to access PrairieTest.)

However, firewalling isn't enough to deliver secure exams.  
Specifically, our requirements for a secure exam are:

\begin{enumerate}

\item The exam can only be accessed while inside one of the CBTF
  spaces.  Canvas, our campus LMS, supports this constraint.

\item An exam can only be accessed by a particular student during
    the specific time of that student's reservation for that exam.  (That is, a
    student cannot make a reservation for exam A and use the
    opportunity to peek at exam B.)  Canvas does not gracefully
    support this constraint except by manually editing each student's
    allowed access-time window.

 \item  Other course materials must not be accessible to the student
      while in the CBTF, except as designated by the instructor,
      whereas students outside the CBTF should be able to access those
      materials as usual.  Canvas does not support this constraint at all.

\end{enumerate}

Some colleagues have reported using lockdown browsers such as
Respondus, but this leads to (at least) two problems, in addition to
the fundamental problem that such a browser is essentially spyware.
First, a lockdown browser must be installed and configured to work
with a particular LMS,
so there is an $M\times N$ problem of licensing and installing a browser
to work with each LMS that could be used to administer exams.  Second,
if the LMS itself is not designed for secure delivery, careful
attention must be paid to configuring the lockdown browser to do this
job: for example, it must restrict access to course materials, but not
to secondary sites
that are actually needed for the exam, such as those that serve images
related to the exam
or provide an embedded coding environment.
We judged this
Whac-A-Mole\footnote{Whac-A-Mole is a registered trademark (1975) of
Bob's Space Racers, Inc. Really.} to be unproductive and risky.

The correct solution is therefore a combination of a properly configured IP
firewall and an application that is actually
designed to deliver secure assessments (as defined by the above
requirements).  PrairieLearn is correctly designed for this use case.
Hence we created an institutional CBTF-specific login profile whose
password is publicly known; that
profile is only available for login inside CBTF rooms, is configured with  the
necessary firewall rules, and is the only profile from which in-CBTF exams are
accessible at all.

\subsection{Hardware: Ancient Legacy Desktops}

\lesson{15-year-old desktop computers in
  underutilized computer labs have a new lease on life.}

The shared labs' computers were several generations old and running a legacy OS (Windows~10),
which turned out to be fine, 
since PrairieLearn exams require only a browser on the client side.
Given the legacy OS, 
it was fortunate that 
PrairieLearn adheres to widely-adopted (and therefore
legacy-compatible) standards for both 
the browser GUI and, importantly, accessibility.
(When we acquired dedicated CBTF space after the pilot, 
we purchased mini-PCs online for under \$500 each, fully equipped
including keyboards and monitors.)

The CBTF-specific login profile mentioned above disables
software installation, filesystem access, and USB ports
(to prevent students bringing USB-borne materials
to an exam), and has virtually no software installed other than a Web
browser and some audio files of white noise for students
who want to block out audio distractions.
We use the open source Clonezilla to uniformly image all machines, and
DeepFreeze software to restore the computers to a pristine 
state upon logout. This prevents the leaking of
any data (including cookies, files, etc.) across exam sessions.

We tried to maintain 10-15\% spare hardware capacity ($2$ idle
machines each room) in case of hardware malfunction during an exam.


\subsection{Authentication: Dry Run It First}
\lesson{Two-factor authentication (2FA) can present subtle pitfalls in
  a CBTF environment.}

Most software systems integrated at our institution, including PrairieLearn, support
single sign-on (SSO) login via our Shibboleth-based institutional authentication
system.
However, as a matter of campus policy, most
SSO-accessible sites can trigger a 2FA flow%
%\footnote{\url{https://calnet.berkeley.edu/calnet-2-step}}
\footnote{\url{URL blinded for review}}
to complete login.
The most common way to do so is to respond to a challenge on one's
cell phone via a particular app (Cisco Duo in our case), but there are
other ways as well.  The 2FA requirement led to
various failure modes:

\begin{itemize}

\item Wifi and/or cellular data coverage is weak in the basement where the labs are, so the phone
  cannot complete the 2FA flow.
\item The student recently obtained a new cell phone and has not yet
  installed and linked the 2FA app.
\item The student normally uses a different method to complete 2FA,
  such as a physical USB token, tablet, or touchID-capable device,
  none of which are permitted in the CBTF.

\end{itemize}

The correct solution for us in our full-time CBTF is to disable 2FA
for logins on the CBTF's CIDR block.  But this is possible only with
close cooperation with campus IT (happily, the team responsible for
2FA and the team responsible for maintaining the shared instructional
labs are in the same campus unit) and because the full-time CBTF's
computers have their own CIDR block.  In one of our still-shared CBTF
spaces where we cannot disable 2FA, we allow students to have their
devices for the first 10 minutes of each exam slot in case needed to
complete login; at the 10-minute mark, before the proctor ``opens''
the exam in PrairieTest, all devices must be placed at the front of
the room.
