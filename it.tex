\section{Computing Infrastructure}

\subsection{Software: Don't Self-Host}
\label{sec:software}
\lesson{Don't self-host. The expected cost
  savings evaporates with other overheads, and you won't get PrairieTest.}

Although 
Canvas\footnote{instructure.com/canvas} is our official
campuswide Learning Management System, we found it impoverished 
for both assessment authoring and assessment
delivery (Section~\ref{sec:networking} describes the latter).
In contrast, we found much to admire about 
PrairieLearn\footnote{prairielearn.org} and
intended to use it.
Indeed, our pilot course was among the first at our institution to use
PrairieLearn,
having converted its five 1-hour quizzes to that format during the
COVID-19 pandemic.
Because our pilot consisted of a single 230-student course,
we initially hosted PrairieLearn ourselves, since it was open source
and we didn't know if we'd need to make changes to integrate with our
campus systems.

In retrospect, this was a mistake.
Our IT staff are highly competent and very cooperative.  However,
PrairieLearn represented a brand-new set of tools and
workflows for them, and their time and budget are tight.
Even with light usage, smooth operation during exams is
mission-critical and required ``on-call'' time from our already busy
IT staff to scale cloud capacity manually when large exams were coming up.
Cloud provisioning was further complicated by having to manage Docker
containers for custom autograders, on which our CS course relied heavily.
Finally, when we had trouble with either system operations or
using a feature correctly,
we had no ``tech
support'':
the PrairieLearn community (on Slack) and the PrairieLearn.com staff were
extremely helpful, but
they had no visibility into our configuration to help
troubleshoot specific problems.
PrairieLearn
hosting would have eliminated all of these concerns.
\footnote{As a caveat, as of this writing, a course requiring custom
external grading containers costs twice as much per hosted seat as one that doesn't.}

\label{sec:reservations}
Finally, the open-source software does not include PrairieTest,
which facilitates
scheduling the CBTF itself and allowing students to make exam
reservations.
Again, we assumed that with only one course needing exam scheduling
and two shared lab spaces, we'd be able to use free tools such as
SignupGenius\footnote{SignupGenius.com}.  This was also a mistake.
Even ``simple'' exam scheduling quickly becomes complicated when one
doesn't use purpose-built tools.
First, students with extra-time accommodations must be handled via a
separate signup, since most tools have no way of allowing only a certain
known group of individuals to reserve longer timeslots (if in fact
those individuals have to authenticate themselves to make a
reservation at all, and if the authentication can be somehow mapped to
their campus identity).
Second, students with reduced-distraction accommodations must be
handled separately, whether using the traditional (for us) method of filling
a room to only $1/3$ or $1/2$ capacity or the reduced-distraction test
station configuration we developed later.
PrairieTest would have solved both problems out-of-the-box.

\subsection{Network: Not Just Firewalls}
\label{sec:networking}

\lesson{Firewalls aren't enough for secure assessment delivery:
  it also requires the LMS to be secure, and most aren't.}

Since each instructional lab had its own subnet, it was easy to
configure  the campus-level firewall to
lock out most of the
Internet when accessed from one of the labs, except for the servers
needed to access PrairieLearn exams. 
One
caveat regarding the firewall configuration was that attempting to access
any blocked site resulted in the same error message one would see if
there were no Internet connection at all.
Some students would make a typo in accessing PrairieLearn or
PrairieTest, see this error, and report to the proctor that their
computer had no Internet.  (This occurred numerous times despite the
fact that upon signing in, 
there was an immediate and obvious desktop
shortcut that students were instructed to use to access PrairieTest.)

However, firewalling isn't enough to deliver secure exams.  
Specifically, our requirements for a secure exam are:

\begin{enumerate}

\item The exam can only be accessed while inside one of the CBTF
  spaces.  Canvas supports this constraint.

\item A student cannot make a reservation for exam A and use the
    opportunity to peek at exam B.  Canvas does not gracefully
    support this constraint except by manually editing each student's
    allowed access-time window for both A and B.

 \item  Non-exam course materials must simultaneously be accessible to
   students outside the CBTF but inaccessible to students in the CBTF.
  Canvas does not support this constraint.

\end{enumerate}

Some colleagues have reported using lockdown browsers such as
Respondus to secure other LMSs, but this leads to (at least) two problems.
First, a lockdown browser must be licensed, installed, and configured to work
with each particular LMS---an $M\times N$ problem.  Second,
having the lockdown browser ``plug holes'' left open by the LMS is error-fraught:
for example, it must restrict access to course materials, but not
to secondary sites
that are actually needed for the exam, such as those that serve images
related to the exam
or provide an embedded coding environment.
We judged this
Whac-A-Mole\footnote{Whac-A-Mole is a registered trademark (1975) of
Bob's Space Racers, Inc. Really.}-like process to be unproductive and risky.

The correct solution is therefore a combination of a properly configured 
firewall \emph{and\/} an application 
designed to deliver  assessments securely, as PrairieLearn is.
All we had to do was create an institutional CBTF-specific login profile whose
password is publicly known; that
profile is only available for login inside CBTF rooms, is configured with  the
necessary firewall rules, and is the only profile from which in-CBTF exams are
accessible at all.

\subsection{Hardware: Ancient Legacy Desktops}

\lesson{Don't throw away old desktops, but do make sure your
  software likes them.}

The shared labs' computers were several generations old and running
Windows~10, largely because of the Microsoft-centric
infrastructure for imaging and maintaining computers across several
centrally-managed labs.   
Fortunately, PrairieLearn requires only a browser on the client side
so it is
largely OS-agnostic, and it adheres to widely-adopted (and therefore
legacy-friendly) standards for both 
the browser GUI and accessibility.
When we later acquired dedicated CBTF space, 
we purchased mini-PCs online for under \$500 each, fully equipped.

The CBTF-specific Windows login profile disables
software installation, filesystem access, and USB ports
(to prevent students bringing USB-borne materials
to an exam), and has virtually no software installed other than a Web
browser and some audio files of white noise for students
who want to block out audio distractions.
We use DeepFreeze\footnote{https://www.faronics.com/products/deep-freeze}
to restore each computer to a pristine state upon logout to prevent
leaking of cookies, files, and so forth across exam sessions.

When scheduling reservations, we always left 1 or 2 machines free in
each room in case of hardware malfunction during an exam, which
happened once or twice.


\subsection{Authentication: Dry Run It First}
\lesson{Two-factor authentication (2FA) can present subtle pitfalls in
  a CBTF environment.}

Most software systems integrated at our institution, including PrairieLearn, support
single sign-on (SSO) login via our Shibboleth-based institutional authentication
system.
However, as a matter of campus policy, most
SSO-accessible sites can trigger a 2FA flow
to complete login.
The most common way to do so is to respond to a challenge on one's
cell phone via a particular auth app, but there are
other ways as well.  The 2FA requirement led to
various failure modes when students tried to login for an exam:

\begin{itemize}

\item Wifi and/or cellular data coverage is weak in the basement where the labs are, so the phone
  cannot complete the 2FA flow.
\item The student recently obtained a new cell phone and has not yet
  configured the 2FA app.
\item The student normally uses a different method to complete 2FA,
  such as a physical USB token, tablet, or touchID-capable device,
  none of which are permitted in the CBTF.

\end{itemize}

The correct solution is to disable 2FA
for logins on the CBTF's subnet, but this requires
close cooperation with campus IT (happily, the responsible team
is in the same unit as the team that maintains the instructional labs)
and is only possible in a dedicated CBTF space.
In one of our still-shared CBTF
spaces where we cannot disable 2FA, we allow students to have their
devices for the first 10 minutes of each exam slot in case needed to
complete login; at the 10-minute mark, before the proctor ``opens''
the exam in PrairieTest, all devices must be placed at the front of
the room.
