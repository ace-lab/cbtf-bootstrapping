\section{Related Work}

The context of our project was a desire to leverage technology to bring the benefits of
Bloom's ``mastery learning''~\cite{Bloom68}, as operationalized by Garcia et al.'s ``A's for All''
philosophy~\cite{garcia-asforall}, to our large CS courses and
ultimately the campus without creating large burdens on course staff.
In 2020, we had initiated a research project to pivot
more campus courses towards mastery learning, an approach that
had been informally gaining popularity in our large gateway Computer
Science courses. A key component of mastery learning is frequent and
short summative
assessments that afford some flexibility in when to take them and the
possibility of re-takes to demonstrate mastery on earlier
material~\cite{secondchanceexams}.  We believed that the extensive and
successful experience reported by the University of Illinois in
efficienty operating Computer-Based Testing
Facilities~\cite{zilles-testing-less-trying} was the most promising
approach to addressing that aspect of mastery learning.

Downey et al.~\cite{downey-cbtf} recently reported their experience
setting up a similar center at the University of California,
Riverside.  Our goals and experience are comparable to theirs.  Their
experience report was written after operating their testing center for
two academic quarters; this one is written with the benefit of over
two years of hindsight and significant scale-up, and we hope the
concrete lessons reported here---especially those regarding
accessibility workflows, funding, and managing messaging and policy
issues---serve as an additional useful resource to others pursuing
similar projects.
