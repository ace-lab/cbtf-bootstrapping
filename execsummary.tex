
\lesson{
  \textbf{Key Lesson 1:}
  Avoid adopting
  processes that won't scale.  Exceptional situations should create $O(1)$ work per
  student or per instructor rather than $O(n)$ work for CBTF
  proctors/staff.

  \textbf{Key Lesson 2:}
  Various campus units typically ``own'' lab facilities, proctoring
  for students with disabilities, instructional software and hardware
  maintenance, room reservations, and so on; in all likelihood, 
  no one owns exam administration.  You will have to create new
  administrative processes, disrupt existing ones, correct mistaken
  faculty assumptions, and coordinate across nonporous administrative
  boundaries.
}

\section{Executive Summary}


In 2020, some of us at \inst{} initiated a project to pivot
more campus courses towards mastery learning [cite], an approach that
had been gaining popularity in our large lower division Computer
Science courses. A key component of mastery learning is frequent and
short summative
assessments that afford some flexibility in when to take them and the
possibility of re-takes to demonstrate mastery on earlier material
[cite].  To achieve that goal, we aimed to emulate the very successful
approach of operationally streamlined computer-based testing
facilities (CBTFs) as demonstrated for several years at the University of Illinois at
Urbana-Champaign [cite Craig’s paper], which differ significantly from
generalized proctoring facilities~\cite{fox-cbtf-not-proctoring}.

Like Downey et al.~\cite{downey-cbtf}, with whose experience ours has
much in common,
our goal was to support testing for entire courses,
not just students with
special proctoring needs.  
Because we believed a testing center should be
a common good for supporting mastery learning, if the pilot succeeded we planned to expand to a
campus-level service.  This would
mean aggressively courting courses beyond the College of Engineering, 
especially the large gateway courses ($n~1000$) that would
benefit most from mastery learning due to uneven student preparation;
turning over CBTF operations to a central
campus unit; and developing funding and governance models for this
cross-unit effort that did not
currently exist.


The course chosen for our  ``proto-CBTF''  pilot
was an upper-division
software systems course enrolling around 250 students.
It was among the first at our institution to use
PrairieLearn for its assessments (we
justify this choice in Section~\ref{sec:software}),
having converted its five 1-hour quizzes to that format during the
COVID-19 pandemic.
(Various other courses had migrated their formative assessments to PrairieLearn, but were later in
adopting CBTF-based summative assessments.)
The pilot course's quizzes  were taken remotely using
videoconference-based proctoring, 
but once the pandemic lockdown ended
and the instructors had some
operational experience with PrairieLearn,
the next step was to convert the 2.5-hour final exam
and run it in a prototype CBTF.
That is the experience
this paper describes.  
(The quizzes remained remote, which was a mistake but forced in
part by the limited resources for the pilot.  
Ironically, the higher
academic integrity of the CBTF-based final 
revealed cheating on the quizzes, as we describe in Section~\ref{sec:cheating}.)

Describe the SEA model

The final exam was administered to 217 students in our proto-CBTF over
a four-day period with two to three 3-hour sessions available for reservation each day,
for a total of ten sessions.

Compare the prototype to full-blown SEA.


A separate report~\cite{fox-cbtf-scaling}
describes our experience scaling up from the prototype described here to our
current scenario: a 30-seat dedicated CBTF with a 100-seat CBTF
opening in Fall 2026, currently serving over 20 courses and over 5,500
enrolled students. 
This report identifies the lessons learned during the pilot that made
the scale-up successful, calling out the ones that turned out to be
especially important.
