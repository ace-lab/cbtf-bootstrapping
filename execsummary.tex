\lesson{
  \textbf{Takeaway 1:}
  Even in a small pilot, avoid adopting
  processes that won't scale.  Exceptional situations should create $O(1)$ work per
  student or per instructor rather than $O(n)$ work for CBTF staff.

  \textbf{Takeaway 2:}
  Various campus units typically ``own'' lab facilities, proctoring
  for students with disabilities, instructional software and hardware
  maintenance, room reservations, and so on; in all likelihood, 
  no one owns exam administration.  You will have to create new
  administrative processes, disrupt existing ones, correct mistaken
  faculty assumptions, and coordinate across nonporous administrative
  boundaries.
}

\section{Executive Summary}


In Fall 2023, we ran a pilot of a Computer-Based Testing Facility
(CBTF) inspired by the extensive experience with CBTFs as pioneered at
the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign~\cite{zilles-testing-less-trying} and
by another institution's experience trying to emulate
it~\cite{downey-cbtf}. 
Like those institutions, our goal was to support proctored
computer-based testing
for entire courses, not just for students with special accommodations.
Because we believed a testing center should be
a common good for supporting mastery learning, if the pilot succeeded we planned to expand to a
campus-level service.  This would
require demonstrating the utility of the service beyond the College of Engineering, 
turning over CBTF operations to a centrally resourced
campus unit, and developing funding and governance models for this
cross-unit effort that did not
currently exist.
This paper describes our experience and challenges
bootstrapping this effort; some of the lessons became evident only with a certain
amount of hindsight.

The course chosen for our ``proto-CBTF'' pilot was an upper-division
software/systems course enrolling around 230 students.
It was among the first at our institution to use PrairieLearn for its
assessments (we justify this choice in Section~\ref{sec:software}),
having converted its five 1-hour quizzes to that format during the
COVID-19 pandemic.
(Various other courses had migrated their formative assessments to
PrairieLearn, but were later in adopting CBTF-based summative
assessments.)
The pilot course's quizzes were taken remotely using
videoconference-based proctoring, but once the pandemic lockdown ended
and the instructors had some operational experience with PrairieLearn,
the next step was to convert the 2.5-hour final exam and run it in a
prototype CBTF in Fall 2023.
That experience is the primary basis of this paper.

A key property of the ``UIUC model'' we were trying to emulate
is that the exam has enough randomness built into it that  not all
students take it simultaneously---a necessity since we had only about
55 seats in our proto-CBTF spaces, and a boon to students since they could
self-schedule their exam over a multi-day window.
The model also embodies other
practices that are important at scale but could  have been ignored for our single course;
since we planned to scale, we tried to avoid scale-thwarting
practices, indicating in the text
where we would have done things differently in hindsight.

We divide the experience into three categories: physical space and
configuration (Section~\ref{sec:physical}), IT infrastructure
(Section~\ref{sec:it}), and policies/coordination/communication (Section~\ref{sec:policy}).
